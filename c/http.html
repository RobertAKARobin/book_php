Computers talk to each other one bit at a time. Computer memory is made of tiny little switches that are either "on" or "off", like light switches. Each bit is an electric signal that tells your computer to turn one of those switches on or off. This changes how electricity flows through your computer and makes it do something like change the color of a pixel on its screen.

Your computer displays webpages because another computer tells it to turn its switches on and off in a certain way, which makes the pixels on your screen light up a certain way, which makes a pattern of lights that your eyes see as a webpage.

Computers talk to each other really fast. Verizon and Comcast might advertise 50 Mbps download speeds, which means your computer is getting 50 mega-bits per second. One mega-bit is 1,000 bits. One letter of the alphabet usually takes up 8 bits (called a byte), so 50 Mbps works out to over 6,000 letters per second.

This system of talking is called HTTP. The 'P' stands for 'Protocol'. That's why you see http:// before so many web addresses: it tells your web browser to talk to other computers with HTTP. (There are a bunch of other Internet protocols but you'll never see most of them.)

You don't need to say h-t-t-p-colon-slash-slash when you're telling someone a web address. Web browsers add in the http:// automatically, so saying that is like saying someone's last name every single time you say their first name.
