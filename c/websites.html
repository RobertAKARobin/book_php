In 5 minutes Will Smith is supposed to go on stage and perform a new rap song. But he has a bad cold and can't talk. It's too late to cancel the concert. I have to take his place.

I don't know the lyrics, so I dial 976-4841 (that's WSMITH-1). Will's secretary picks up. I say, Read me the lyrics for Will's new song. She goes to her filing cabinet, gets out a folder marked Raps, flips through it for a document labled New, and reads it over the phone to me.

I memorize the lyrics she read me, go on stage, and perform the song.

Replace Will's secretary with a computer program called a server, and my phone with a web browser (like Google Chrome, Mozilla Firefox, Apple Safari, or Internet Explorer), and you've basically got how a website works.

You enter www.wsmith1.com/raps/new.html into your web browser's address bar. Your computer "dials" wsmith1.com. A server at wsmith1.com picks up and sees that you want it to look in a folder called raps for a file called new.html. It reads the file back to your computer, and your computer displays the file on your screen.

Every webpage is a file on a computer somewhere. That file's kept in a folder. That's all any website is — a bunch of files and folders, just like the files and folders on your personal computer.
