In 5 minutes, Will Smith is supposed to go on stage in front of a million people and perform a new rap song. But he has a bad cold and can't talk, and it's too late to cancel the show. I have to take his place.

I don't know the lyrics. We've decided that I'll go on stage, call his secretary on my phone, she'll read the lyrics to me, and I'll rap them.

I dial 976-4841 (that's WSMITH-1). His secretary picks up. I say, Read me the lyrics for Will's new song. She goes to a filing cabinet, gets out a folder marked Raps, flips through it for a piece of paper labled New, and reads it over the phone to me while I perform it.

Replace Will's secretary with a computer program called a server, and my phone with a web browser (like Google Chrome, Mozilla Firefox, Apple Safari, or Internet Explorer), and you've basically got how a website works.

You enter www.wsmith1.com/raps/new.html into your web browser's address bar. Your computer "dials" wsmith1.com. A server at wsmith1.com picks up and sees that you want it to look in a folder called raps for a file called new.html. It reads the file back to your computer, and your computer displays the file on your screen.

Every webpage is a file on a computer somewhere. That file's kept in a folder. That's all any website is — a bunch of files and folders, just like the files and folders on your personal computer.

If your computer was big enough to hold all of Amazon.com's files, and Amazon let you download them, you could browse Amazon on your computer the way you browse your school essays or cat photos.
