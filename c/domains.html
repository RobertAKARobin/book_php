Every machine connected to the Internet has its own "phone number," called an IP address. Your computer has one. So does the computer that runs my website (my server). To view my website, people need that number. But 216.194.168.37 is hard to remember, so instead I tell people my website's domain, robertakarobin.com, which is easier to remember. This is sort-of like how Will's phone number is actually 976-4841, but he tells people to dial WSMITH-1.

When you enter robertakarobin.com in your web browser, that request is sent to a special computer called a nameserver. Its job is to look up a domain's IP address in the same way you'd look up Will Smith's phone number: by searching the phone book for Will Smith. The nameserver looks through a bunch of records, sees that robertakarobin.com has the IP address 216.194.168.37, and then tells your computer to send its request there. This all happens under-the-hood.

So WSMITH-1 is to 976-4841 as a domain is to an IP address in that it keeps people from having to remember a huge number.

Will Smith is to 976-4841 as a domain is to an IP address in that he "owns" that huge number and is connected to people through it.

This Internet "phone book" is called the DNS (Domain Name System). When you set up a website the DNS is updated to show what IP address belongs to your domain. That update is sent to (or "propogated among") all the nameservers in the world. This lets people view your website without having to memorize your IP address.
